6. Usage

Parallel coordinates are used for identifying outliers and clusters among records, and discovering correlations between dimensions.

6.1 Identifying Outliers Among Records

An outlier is a record whose data points differ significantly from other records. Outliers stand out clearly, because their polylines follow a different path to those of other records.

The Heart Health Dataset contains one obvious outlier, Patient F, with significantly higher blood pressure and cholesterol, lower heart rate, high BMI, and advanced age.

6.2 Identifying Clusters of Records

Clusters are groups of data records which have similar characteristics, so their polylines follow similar paths.

6.3 Discovering Correlations Between Dimensions

If two dimensions are positively correlated, high values in one dimension correspond to high values in the other. Low values in one dimension correspond to low values in the other. In a parallel coordinates chart, this can be seen only when the two dimensions are adjacent to one another.

A perfect positive correlation is characterised by parallel, non-crossing straight line segments running betwen the two dimensions, as shown in Figure 1a. In a scatterplot, this corresponds to points falling along a straight line, as shown in Figure 1b. In real life, data is typically messier, and a positive correlation can be less obvious in parallel coordinates than in a scatterplot, as shown in Figures 2a and 2b.

If two dimensions are negatively correlated, low values in one correspond to high values in the other and vice versa. A perfect negative correlation is characterised by line segments between adjacent dimensions which all cross in the middle, as shown in Figure 3a. In the equivalent scatterplot, the points fall along a negatively inclined straight line, as shown in Figure 3b.
