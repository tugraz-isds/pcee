6. Usage

Parallel coordinates are used for identifying outliers and clusters among records, and discovering correlations between dimensions.

The Heart Health Dataset is a fictitious, curated dataset representing health-related measurements. It consists of 8 dimensions (Patient, Age, Blood Pressure, Heart Rate, BMI, Cholesterol, Fitness Score, and Treatment) and 6 records (for six patients).

6.1 Identifying Outliers Among Records

An outlier is a record whose data points differ significantly from other records in one or more dimensions. Outliers stand out clearly, because their polylines follow a different path to those of other records. 6.3 Discovering Correlations Between Dimensions

If two dimensions are positively correlated, high values in one dimension correspond to high values in the other, and low values in that dimension correspond to low values in the other. In a parallel coordinates chart, this can be seen only when the two dimensions are adjacent to one another.

A positive correlation is characterised by fairly parallel, rarely crossing straight line segments running between the two dimensions, as shown in Figure 2a. In a scatterplot, this corresponds to points falling close to a straight line, as shown in Figure 2b.

In the Heart Health Dataset, the dimensions Age and Blood Pressure are positively correlated. Tendentially, blood pressure increases with age. With real data, parallel non-crossing line segments are rare. It often makes sense to invert one of the two dimensions to check for the visual cross pattern. If two dimensions are negatively correlated, low values in one correspond to high values in the other and vice versa. A negative correlation is characterised by a visual cross pattern between adjacent dimensions, i.e. line segments which all cross in the middle, as shown in Figure 3a. In the equivalent scatterplot, the points fall close to a negatively inclined straight line, as shown in Figure 3b.

6.4 Beware of False Patterns

A categorical dimension is typically ordered alphabetically when displayed in parallel coordinates. This can sometimes suggest a pattern which is not really there. For example, in the Heart Health Dataset, it appears that Patient (name) is inversely correlated with Age. This is probably simply an artefact of the alphabetical ordering.
